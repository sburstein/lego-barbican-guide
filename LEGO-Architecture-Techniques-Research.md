# LEGO Architecture Techniques: Deep Research for Brutalist Builds

Compiled from Tom Alphin's work, BrickNerd, Brick Architect, New Elementary, community builders, and multiple MOC sources.

---

## 1. TOM ALPHIN'S "THE LEGO ARCHITECT" — KEY PRINCIPLES

Tom Alphin (Brick Architect, author of *The LEGO Architect*, No Starch Press 2015) covers 7 architectural styles with buildable models. His Brutalism chapter specifically addresses concrete massing.

**What makes his builds look professional:**

- **Monochromatic discipline.** Each style uses a restricted palette — Brutalism in greys and whites only. Color restriction forces you to rely on form, shadow, and texture rather than decoration.
- **Massing first, detail second.** He designs the overall silhouette/volume before any surface treatment. The model should read as the building from across a room.
- **Real architectural proportions.** He sketches elevations with actual stud/plate measurements before building. Floor heights stay consistent (typically 3 bricks = 1 floor at his scale).
- **Destroy and rebuild.** His Geisel Library took multiple attempts (~800 pieces final). The process is iterative — build, photograph, tear down, rebuild better.

**Geisel Library MOC specifics:**
- ~800 bricks, ~9 inches wide
- Used 4x4 tiled roof sections for the stepped cube forms
- Built 3/4/5 Pythagorean triangle bracing for angled "concrete" supports
- Translated the cantilevered reading rooms as overhanging plate stacks

**His Brutalist-specific advice:**
- Brutalism = celebrate massive angular forms in reinforced concrete
- LEGO's orthogonal nature is a *feature* for Brutalism, not a limitation
- Use 1x1 slope pieces in grey for window details
- Translucent clear bricks for glazing between concrete masses
- 1x2 palisade bricks (part 30136) and 1x2 profile bricks for surface variation between housing units

## 2. WHAT DISTINGUISHES IMPRESSIVE ARCHITECTURE STUDIO BUILDS

**The Set (21050):** 1,210 pieces, 76 different white/transparent elements, 272-page guidebook endorsed by REX, Sou Fujimoto, SOM, MAD Architects, Safdie Architects.

**Key inventory limitations to know:**
- NO white SNOT brackets in the set
- LIMITED curved pieces
- Heavy on basic plates and bricks, light on specialty elements
- To build every model in Alphin's book you need ~252 additional parts (~$25 on BrickLink)
- White color varies slightly across elements (watch for cream vs. bright white)

**What separates impressive builds from simple ones:**

| Simple Builds | Impressive Builds |
|---|---|
| Flat walls, studs visible | SNOT facades, smooth tiled surfaces |
| Single-plane facades | Multiple depth layers (2-3 planes) |
| Uniform brick stacking | Mixed plate/brick/tile textures |
| No underside treatment | Inverted slopes for soffits |
| Blocky silhouette | Deliberate cantilevers and overhangs |
| Random proportions | Consistent floor heights, aligned windows |
| Monolithic mass | Ground floor / mid-facade / roofline hierarchy |

**Professional detail hierarchy (from How to Build It MOC):**
1. **Ground floor** — distinctive features, entrances, columns
2. **Mid-facade** — rhythmic window pattern with subtle variation
3. **Roofline** — cornices, mechanical screens, silhouette definition

**The "window module" approach:** Pick a repeating window unit (e.g. "1x4 trans-clear + 1x2 grille + 1x1 round plate") and repeat with subtle variations. Rhythm + variation = professional read.

## 3. SNOT TECHNIQUES FOR ARCHITECTURE — COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE

### The Critical Geometry Rules

**5:2 Rule:** 2 studs wide = 5 plates tall (with exposed studs)
**6:5 Rule:** 6 studs wide = 5 bricks tall (with exposed stud)
**Single stud:** 1 stud width = 2.5 plates height

These ratios are the foundation of all SNOT alignment.

### Essential SNOT Parts for Architecture

| Part # | Name | What It Does |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **4070** | Headlight Brick 1x1 | The classic. Stud on top + stud on side. Exactly 2 plates thick. The recessed back accepts a plate. Cornerstone of facade detailing. |
| **87087** | 1x1 Brick, Stud on 1 Side | Clean single side-stud. Use for attaching tiles/plates to facade face. |
| **4733** | Travis Brick (1x1, Studs on 4 Sides) | Stud in every direction. Use as hub for corner details. |
| **11211** | 1x2 Brick, Stud on 1 Side | Wider version of 87087. Two attachment points on top, one stud facing out. |
| **30414** | 1x4 Brick, Stud on 1 Side | Long SNOT brick. Excellent for attaching full facade panels. |
| **47905** | 1x1 Brick, Studs on 2 Adjacent Sides | Corner piece — two perpendicular studs. Essential for building around corners in SNOT. |
| **26604** | 1x1 Brick, Studs on 2 Adjacent Sides | Updated version of corner stud brick. |

### Brackets (the half-plate offset masters)

| Part # | Name | Key Property |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **99781** | 1x2 - 1x2 Down Bracket | Half-plate-thick extension pointing DOWN. Creates recessed plane. |
| **99780** | 1x2 - 1x2 Up Bracket | Same but pointing UP. |
| **36841** | 1x1 - 1x1 Bracket | Smallest bracket. Tight spaces. |
| **44728** | 2x2 - 2x2 Bracket | Large area coverage. |
| **2436** | 1x2 - 1x4 Bracket | Asymmetric — good for offset facade panels. |

**Critical bracket property:** Bracket extensions are exactly **half a plate thick**. This means when you attach tiles to brackets, your facade surface sits at a half-plate offset from the wall behind it. This is how you create subtle reveals.

### Jumper Plates (half-stud offsets)

| Part # | Name | Use |
|--------|------|-----|
| **15573** | 1x2 Jumper | Single centered stud. Half-stud offset in one direction. |
| **87580** | 2x2 Jumper | Centered stud on 2x2. Half-stud offset both directions. |

**Architecture application:** Jumper plates let you center windows in odd-width bays and create asymmetric tapers on towers (as used on the LEGO John Hancock Center).

### SNOT Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Headlight + 87087 via 1x2 plate** = visible gap and stress. Don't mix these directly.
- **Technic halfpins (4274) in SNOT** sit 0.1mm higher than standard SNOT studs. If you use one Technic connection, use them throughout that section.
- **Tolerance stacking:** Sideways connections include 0.1mm tolerance per brick side. Over many bricks, this accumulates. Keep SNOT sections modest in span.

## 4. BRUTALIST-SPECIFIC TECHNIQUES

### Representing Raw/Bush-Hammered Concrete Texture

**Method 1: Exposed Stud Texture**
The simplest approach — leave studs exposed on a light bluish grey (LBG) wall. At small scale, regular stud pattern reads as aggregate concrete texture. This is genuinely effective and the dominant technique among Brutalist LEGO builders.

**Method 2: Mixed Surface Technique (the 80/20 rule)**
- 80% primary surface type (smooth tiles OR studs-up)
- 20% contrasting texture for emphasis
- Scatter 1x1 round plates and 1x1 bricks with side studs (87087) randomly into a studs-up grey wall for a pockmarked, weathered concrete look
- Transition zones: use 1x1 pieces scattered randomly rather than linear boundaries

**Method 3: Profile/Palisade Bricks**
- Part **98283** (1x2 Masonry Profile Brick) — has a sculpted face suggesting stone/concrete blocks
- Part **30136** (1x2 Palisade Brick) — log-cabin profile, reads as board-formed concrete at small scale
- Mix these into otherwise smooth walls for textural variation

**Method 4: Color Gradient for Weathering**
For concrete aging effects, graduate through:
- Light Bluish Grey (base concrete)
- Medium Stone Grey (weathered)
- Dark Bluish Grey (water staining in recesses)
- White (mineral deposits/efflorescence)

Use 1x1 pieces to create soft transitions. Focus weathering around joints and water-collection points.

### Deep-Set Windows with Reveals

**The Core Technique:** Don't recess the windows — project the walls forward.

**Step-by-step (from BrickNerd skyscraper tutorial):**

1. Build your structural wall as normal (studs up or sideways)
2. Between window openings, attach **1x1 bricks with side stud (87087)** or **1x1 with 2 adjacent studs (26604)** at corners
3. Attach **1x6 tiles** (or appropriate length tiles) vertically to the side studs
4. The tiles now sit FORWARD of the window plane by half a plate
5. The window glass (trans-clear plates/bricks) stays at the wall plane
6. Result: 0.5-plate reveal depth between wall surface and glass

**For deeper reveals (full plate depth):**
- Use **30414** (1x4 brick with side stud) or **11211** (1x2 with side stud) as the wall element
- These project a full stud outward
- Attach plates/tiles to the forward-facing studs
- Window glass stays recessed by a full plate behind the facade surface

**For Brutalist deep-set windows specifically:**
- Build a 2-plate-thick outer wall using SNOT
- Leave window openings as voids
- The 2-plate depth automatically creates a shadow reveal
- Line window sides with dark grey or black 1x1 tiles to exaggerate the depth shadow

### Cantilevered Balconies with Visible Soffits

**Upper surface:** Standard plates/tiles extending beyond the wall below

**Soffit (underside) treatment options:**
- **Inverted slopes** angled toward the building create a tapered underside
- Part **3665** (Inverted Slope 45° 2x1) — smallest, most common
- Part **3660** (Inverted Slope 45° 2x2) — wider coverage
- Part **4287** (Inverted Slope 33° 3x1) — shallower, more elegant angle for larger overhangs
- Part **4871** (Inverted Double Slope 45° 4x2) — for substantial soffits on large cantilevers

**Cantilever structural method:**
- For small overhangs (1-2 studs): plates alone, locked by weight above
- For medium overhangs (2-4 studs): use Technic pins through beams as internal reinforcement
- The plate that extends from the back of some inverted slopes provides the structural attachment point

**Key insight:** Inverted slopes serve double duty — they create the angled soffit surface AND contribute structural rigidity through their angled geometry.

### Barrel Vaults at Small Scale

**Method 1: Curved Slope Approximation**
Use curved slope bricks in sequence:
- Part **50950** (Slope Curved 3x1) — most common
- Part **15068** (Slope Curved 2x2) — for wider vaults
Stack these side-by-side across the vault span. At small scale, 3-5 curved slopes across reads as a barrel vault.

**Method 2: Stacked Plates (for very small scale)**
- Stack 1-wide plates, each offset by half a stud using jumper plates
- Creates a stepped curve that reads as vaulted from distance
- Rule: use plates not bricks for smoother gradation

**Method 3: Hinge-Based Curve**
- Use 1x4 hinge plates arranged in an arc
- Mathematics: diameter × π ÷ plate_length = number of plates needed
- For an 18-stud diameter semicircle: ~7 hinge plates
- Works best at medium scale (12+ stud span)

**Method 4: 1x1 Round Brick Hinges**
- Alternate 1x2 or 1x3 plates with 1x1 round bricks
- Round bricks act as pivot points allowing gentle curves
- "Legal" technique (no stress on elements)

### Curved Building Plans Using Rectilinear Pieces

**The 5-plate-height rule:** Curved walls must be multiples of 5 plates high to align with sideways SNOT geometry.

**Gilbert Despathens technique (for limited parts):**
- Build short wall segments (2-3 studs long)
- Connect with 1x2 hinge plates at slight angles
- Each segment can angle 5-15° from the previous
- Smooth the inner face with tiles

**Brick bending (advanced):**
- Standard 1x2 bricks can tolerate slight angular stress
- ~72 bricks per layer for a full circle
- Creates smooth curves but puts elements under stress
- Use sparingly and only for display models

**Best small-scale approach:**
- Faceted polygon using hinge plates
- 8-sided polygon reads as curved from 2+ feet away
- 12-sided polygon is convincingly round
- Tile the outer faces to hide stud joints

## 5. PIECE COUNT SWEET SPOT FOR ARCHITECTURE STUDIO

### The Set's Budget
1,210 pieces total. Based on reviewer experience and Alphin's supplementary parts list:

**Recommended allocation for a single satisfying model:**

| Model Ambition | Piece Count | Dimensions (approx) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Quick study / sketch model | 50-150 | 4-6" footprint | Concept exploration, single facade |
| **Sweet spot: substantial single building** | **200-400** | **6-10" footprint, 4-8" tall** | Enough for SNOT facade, depth, roof detail. Leaves pieces for 1-2 more models. |
| Major showpiece | 500-800 | 10-14" footprint | Consumes most of the set. Alphin's Geisel Library was ~800 pieces. |
| Set-exhausting build | 800-1200 | Large or highly detailed | Leaves nothing for other models |

**The 200-400 piece range is the sweet spot** because:
- Enough pieces for 2-3 facade depth layers
- Can include window reveals, a cantilevered element, and roof detail
- Still leaves 800+ pieces for other models or a second building
- Matches roughly the complexity of official Architecture Skyline individual buildings

**One reviewer built an 11" x 6" x 3" house and still had "plenty of small pieces over."**

### Critical inventory constraints at this scale:
- You have only ~6 white 8x8 plates (guard these for floor/roof slabs)
- Transparent pieces are limited (~40-50 total) — budget windows carefully
- 1x2 jumpers run out fast — one reviewer exhausted them on a 6x10 floor pattern alone

## 6. LAYERED FACADE TECHNIQUES

### The Three-Plane System

Professional LEGO architecture uses up to 3 depth planes:

**Plane 1 (deepest): Window glass**
- Trans-clear plates or bricks sitting at the structural wall plane
- This is the back of your visible facade

**Plane 2 (middle): Wall surface**
- Tiles attached to SNOT bricks (87087, 30414) projecting forward from the structure
- OR bracket-mounted plates creating a half-plate offset
- This is where most of your facade area lives

**Plane 3 (most projecting): Pilasters, cornices, balconies**
- Additional plates/bricks extending beyond Plane 2
- Cornices: stack of plates extending 1 stud beyond wall face
- Balconies: plates cantilevering out from Plane 2 with inverted slope soffits
- Pilasters: 1x1 round bricks or stacked 1x1 plates projecting from wall face

### The Empire State Building Method (from BrickNerd)

This is the most clearly documented layered facade technique:

1. Build structural core (studs up)
2. Attach **87087** (1x1 stud-on-side) bricks at wall sections between windows
3. For corners, use **26604** (1x1, studs on 2 adjacent sides)
4. Attach **1x6 tiles** vertically to the side studs
5. Floor divisions: every 5 plates = one floor (matches 6:5 SNOT ratio)
6. The tiles project forward; window openings remain recessed
7. Result: consistent half-plate reveal across entire facade

### Half-Plate Offset Stack

For a more pronounced depth effect:
1. Full SNOT wall using brackets (**99781** or **99780**)
2. Plates attached to bracket studs form outer skin
3. Tiles cover the outer plates for smooth finish
4. Leave openings in the outer skin for windows
5. The bracket thickness (half plate) plus plate thickness creates a ~1-plate total reveal

### For Brutalist Facades Specifically

Brutalist depth comes from **massive wall thickness** rather than decorative layering:

1. Build walls 2+ plates thick (not just 1-plate SNOT skin)
2. Window openings should be actual voids punched through the full wall thickness
3. The visible reveal = full wall thickness, creating dramatic shadows
4. Balcony slabs should project as visible concrete plates with exposed undersides
5. Use inverted slopes on balcony undersides to show the soffit angle

## 7. INVERTED SLOPE AND OVERHANG TECHNIQUES

### Complete Inverted Slope Reference for Architecture

| Part # | Description | Angle | Best Architectural Use |
|--------|-------------|-------|----------------------|
| **3665** | 2x1 Inverted Slope | 45° | Small cornice returns, window sill undersides |
| **3660** | 2x2 Inverted Slope | 45° | Balcony soffits, medium overhangs |
| **3676** | 2x2 Inverted Corner Slope | 45° | Building corner overhangs |
| **4287** | 3x1 Inverted Slope | 33° | Shallower, more elegant soffit angle |
| **4871** | 4x2 Inverted Double Slope | 45° | Large cantilevered floor soffits |
| **18759** | 3x1 Inverted Double Slope | 45° | Medium soffits, ridge lines |
| **2449** | 2x1x3 Inverted Slope | 75° | Near-vertical undersides, deep cornices |

### How to Build Architectural Overhangs

**Simple Cornice (1-stud overhang):**
```
Top layer:  [====plate extends 1 stud====]
            [inverted slope 3665]
Wall below: [====wall plate====]
```
The inverted slope fills the triangular void under the overhang.

**Cantilevered Floor Slab (2-3 stud overhang):**
```
Upper floor: [===2-plate-thick slab extending 3 studs===]
             [  inverted slope 4871 fills underside    ]
             [  inverted slope 3660 at edge            ]
Wall below:  [===wall===]
```
Use two rows of inverted slopes: the larger 4871 near the wall, smaller 3660 at the edge.

**Visible Concrete Soffit (Brutalist style):**
- Don't fully fill the overhang underside
- Leave the middle as a flat plate underside (the anti-stud surface reads as flat concrete)
- Place inverted slopes only at the transition from wall to overhang
- This creates the characteristic Brutalist "concrete slab floating over concrete slab" look

### Structural Notes
- Inverted slopes themselves add structural rigidity — their angled geometry resists downward forces
- The rear plate extension on parts like 3660 provides the clutch point
- For cantilevers beyond 3 studs: reinforce with Technic axles or pins through the slab above
- Weight the base heavily — cantilevers will topple display models if the center of gravity isn't managed

## 8. ARNDT SCHLAUDRAFF'S APPROACH (3,500+ Brutalist Models)

The most prolific LEGO Brutalist builder offers these insights:

- **White bricks only** — removes all color distraction, forces focus on form and mass
- **3-hour build cycle** — models are built in ~3 hours, photographed, destroyed. Speed forces design clarity.
- **LEGO's orthogonal nature is the point** — the right-angle grid of LEGO naturally produces the crisp geometry of Brutalism
- **Scale figures and model cars** in photographs make LEGO buildings look like real architectural models
- **Limitations are features** — curved forms (Zaha Hadid style) are impossible, so Brutalism's rectilinear forms are the ideal subject matter
- **Photography matters as much as building** — high-contrast lighting, eye-level angles, and tight framing make LEGO buildings read as architecture
